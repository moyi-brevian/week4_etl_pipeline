Why We Must Automate Our Data Pipelines
A one-page note for Operations Leadership | Prepared by Brevian Titus Moyi
The Risk: One Mistyped Number Can Cost a Week
Picture a technician copying pressure and temperature readings from a clipboard into a spreadsheet every
morning. One day, a reading of 35 gets typed as 350 — a simple extra zero. Nobody double-checks it, because
nobody has time to check every number by hand. That one typo can either trigger a false alarm that stops a
line for no reason, or worse, hide a real early warning sign in a wall of numbers that "look fine."
This isn't hypothetical for us. When we reviewed our own plant sensor records recently, we found 166 out of
just over 5,000 readings — about 1 in every 30 — were physically impossible (a temperature colder than
is physically possible, a pressure reading far beyond what any pipe here could survive). None of that was
caught until someone went looking. That's the risk: bad numbers don't announce themselves.
The Solution: A Sprinkler System, Not a Bucket Brigade
A "data pipeline" is simply the everyday path a number travels: someone records it, someone checks it,
someone enters it somewhere useful. Right now, that path runs mostly on people — like carrying water to a
field one bucket at a time, checking each bucket by eye as you go.
An automated pipeline is like switching to a sprinkler system. It draws data straight from the source on a fixed
schedule, and it has a built-in shutoff valve: if the water runs muddy (the data looks wrong), the system stops
itself and says why — instead of spraying the field with dirty water and letting someone find out weeks later.
We still walk the field to spot-check it. We just stop personally hauling every bucket.
The Value: What It Actually Saves Us
Time Manually reviewing and entering 5,000+ readings takes roughly 12–15 hours —
about two full working days — every time it's done. The automated version does
the same job correctly in under one second, every night, without anyone needing
to remember.
Errors In this one dataset, the automated check caught 166 bad readings (about 3% of
the total) before they reached a single report or decision. A person skimming a
spreadsheet at the end of a shift is far more likely to miss a handful of numbers
like that — and it only takes one to trigger the wrong call.
Speed of
decisions
Because the check runs every night, problems surface the next morning — not at
the end of the month during a manual audit. That's the difference between fixing
something this week and explaining it at a quarterly review.
We're not proposing anything exotic — just turning on the sprinkler, so the team's time goes into making
decisions, not typing numbers.
